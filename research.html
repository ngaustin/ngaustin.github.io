My Research

Mission Statement

I work at the intersection of multi-agent reinforcement learning and game theory. I primarily devise game-solving algorithms that minimize computation and data resources. I believe there are several hurdles that need to be addressed before multi-agent learning systems can be introduced and assimilated into the real world, of which I hope to address during my time at the University of Michigan. My research works to expand the reliability, efficiency, and efficacy of multi-agent learning algorithms and ground them in game-theoretic principles.

(Fully) Online Decision Transformer

A good friend of mine and I built upon the recent Decision Transformer for an NLP class at the University of Michigan. We decided to adapt this approach offline, supervised learning approach to online contexts, using a SOTA online RL algorithm as an exploration policy to feed informative behavior to the Decision Transformer for online training. Undergradudate Honors Thesis

Ant-Inspired Decentralized MARL

Ants communicate and coordinate with each other through pheromones, which can induce some amazing joint behavior. This project investigated whether RL agents could learn to do the same: decentralized coordination through pheromone communication.

Agents learned how to modify the environment for traversal through single-agent RL and a coordination algorithm was used to have multiple trained agents cooperatively navigate to a goal location.

Bayesian Conservative Reinforcement Learning

For UC Berkeley's Deep Reinforcement Learning Class (CS 285), a few friends and I combined two recent RL approaches to create a conservative single-agent RL algorithm. Conservatism was achieved by optimizing return CVar, the worst-case return within a specified percentile. This project made me realize that optimizing for average utility might not be advantageous in every scenario. For example, in a driving scenario, it is obviously unlikely to encounter hazards on the road. However, optimizing ML agents in the average case may not account for this situation. As a result, sometimes optimizing for the "worst-case" scenarios are valuable for ML agents.
